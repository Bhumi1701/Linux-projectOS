  Every single day, I lean on Git. It’s a tool I never had to open a wallet for,
  never had to ask some corporate admin for a seat-license to use, and—crucially—one
  I can tear down to its last line of code whenever the mood strikes. That isn't just
  a "feature"; it’s a massive shift in power. It exists only because a handful of
  people decided that building in the open was more important than hoarding IP.

  For me, software freedom is just another word for Sovereignty. Not the "lecture
  hall" version, but the hands-on kind: the right to run, dissect, tweak, and pass it
  on. The FSF’s four freedoms aren't just high-minded ideals—they’re the literal 
  gear-work that keeps technical knowledge from rotting in a silo. Each and every
  open-source technology we make use is living proof of that if I could contribute 
  one thing to this enormous ecosystem it would be a decentralized, peer to peer 
  learning platform. I’d drop it under the GPL v2 specifically to ensure that once
  it’s out there, it stays out there—un-caged and available to anyone. Currently we
  all stand on top of the shoulders of these giants and eventually you have to be the
  shoulders for somebody else. That’s the environment I’m signing up for.
